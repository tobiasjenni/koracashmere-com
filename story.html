Our Story

From the Himalayas
to Your Hands

The circle of Kora — a story of altitude, artisans, and the world's finest fiber.

The Fiber

At 4,000 meters above sea level, winter temperatures drop to −40°C. The Changthangi goat survives by growing an undercoat that engineering cannot replicate — fibers 12 to 15 microns in diameter, finer than the finest merino.

Each spring, nomadic herders comb the goats by hand during the natural molting season. One goat yields approximately 150 grams of raw cashmere per year. A single Kora scarf requires the annual harvest of three goats.

This scarcity is not a marketing story. It is the definition of luxury — something rare because nature made it so.

The Hands

In the Kathmandu Valley, weaving cashmere is not a trade — it is inheritance. The families we work with have been spinning and weaving for three generations. Grandmothers teach daughters, who teach their daughters.

Every Kora piece begins on a traditional charkha — a hand-spinning wheel that has not changed in centuries. The spinner's hands control the tension, the twist, the thickness. No machine can replicate this judgment because no machine can feel the fiber.

One scarf takes approximately eight hours to weave. A blanket takes three days. There are no shortcuts.

The Promise

Kora means "circle" in Nepali — the sacred circumambulation of a mountain. We chose this name because our pieces are designed to complete circles: from goat to hand, from Nepal to you, and eventually from you to the next generation.

We pay our artisan families directly — no middlemen, no agents, no factory owners. Fair wages are not a feature; they are the foundation. When you purchase a Kora piece, you are supporting the preservation of a craft that machines cannot replicate.

Cashmere gets softer with age. Wear them. Pass them on. The circle continues.
