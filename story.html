The Name

KORA

Sacred Circumambulation

In the Tibetan Buddhist tradition, a kora is a walking meditation — a sacred circumambulation around a mountain, a temple, a place of reverence. The walker circles in quiet contemplation, each step an offering, each return to the starting point a deeper understanding.

Our name carries this intention. Every garment we create is a return — to the hands that first touched the fibre, to the mountains that shaped the goat, to the wisdom passed from grandmother to granddaughter across three generations.

To wear KORA is to walk this circle with us.

Our Lineage

Three Generations of Hands

Cashmere weaving in the Kathmandu Valley is not a profession — it is inheritance. Knowledge flows from grandmother to mother to daughter, each generation refining what the last perfected.

1962

The First Thread

Our grandmother, Lhakpa, began combing cashmere goats in the high pastures of Mustang. She sold raw fibre to traders in Kathmandu, dreaming of one day weaving the wool herself. In 1968, she purchased her first hand-loom — the same wooden frame that still sits in our workshop today.

1989

The Workshop Takes Root

Lhakpa's daughter, Pema, expanded the single loom into a small workshop in Bhaktapur. She trained the first three artisans — neighbours who would become lifelong collaborators. Pema insisted on hand-spinning when the industry was racing toward machines. She knew that speed could not replace soul.

2024

KORA Is Born

The third generation — Pema's children — launched KORA to share this heritage with the world. We honour our grandmother's loom and our mother's uncompromising standards. Every KORA piece carries their names in its weave, whether you can see them or not.

The Process

From Mountain to Garment

No machines. No shortcuts. Four deliberate stages — each guided entirely by hand, each a tradition refined over decades in the Kathmandu Valley.

Step 01

Hand-Combing

Each spring, Changthangi goats are gently hand-combed — never sheared. Only the softest underdown fibres, finer than 16 microns, are selected from the raw fleece.

Step 02

Charkha Spinning

On traditional hand-turned charkha wheels, fibres become yarn. This slow rhythm — unchanged for centuries — preserves the natural loft and breathability that machines destroy.

Step 03

Hand-Loom Weaving

Wooden looms, operated entirely by hand and foot, weave yarn into dense, enduring fabric. Each row is pulled tight by feel — a rhythm learned over decades, not programmed into a machine.

Step 04

Hand-Finishing

Every garment is washed in soft Himalayan water, gently stretched to shape, and inspected stitch by stitch. Only when Durga nods does it receive the KORA label.
